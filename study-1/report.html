01

Executive summary

AI is loud on the demand side of travel and quiet on the supply side — and the money is on the quiet side. That single asymmetry is the finding the whole study turns on.

Travellers have adopted AI fast for inspiration and planning: a majority of US travellers have now used AI for a trip, and generative AI accounts for roughly a third of trip research. But adoption collapses at the transaction. Around two-thirds of people will not let AI complete a booking, and OpenAI itself pulled in-chat checkout in March 2026. The result is a market that is enthusiastic upstream, dissatisfied at the point of pain, and structurally blocked at the moment money changes hands.

Meanwhile, on the supply side, AI already pays its way. Revenue-management systems lift ADR/RevPAR by 10–15%, service automation cuts cost by around 30% while automating roughly two-thirds of contacts, and operations tooling is taking real cost out of disruption handling. This is unglamorous, cost-out-as-efficiency work — but it is bankable, measurable, and shipping today.

Assessed on Desirability, Viability and Feasibility, six opportunity zones sort into three tiers. Two clear the gate now, but they are not equal. Supply-side revenue-management and service tooling (Z2) is the firmer anchor: it is bankable today and robust even if the consumer trust gap eventually closes. SME / unmanaged self-serve corporate travel AI (Z1) is a real but more contested fast-follow — and, on closer reading, a take-rate business rather than a software annuity (the clearest comparable, Navan, books only ~$62M of its ~$702M revenue as subscription). Each addresses a serviceable pool of roughly $2–5bn with a proven buyer, and a focused category leader can credibly reach low-hundreds-of-$M ARR within about three years — without depending on the consumer booking-trust gap closing. The rest are gated optionality: in-trip disruption recovery (Z3) is the most desirable moment in the entire data set but the least deliverable; long-tail supply aggregation (Z4) sits on a large pool but is ops-heavy; agentic booking rails (Z5) are strategically large but dated to a 2027+ horizon by the trust gap; the luxury advisor backend (Z6) is a reliable but small niche.

The verdict in one line

GO — the bankable near-term AI-travel bet is supply-side B2B plus SME self-serve, not consumer planning and not agentic booking. The recommendation survives an independent red-team; it sharpens to anchor on Z2, with Z1 as a deliberate, eyes-open second bet. The supporting numbers carry the thesis, not just the narrative.

02

The strategic question

As AI reorganises travel, where is the opportunity large enough, desirable enough, and buildable enough to back?

The study treats that question through three complementary lenses rather than choosing one. Value-chain stages answer where in the journey AI bites. Demand segments answer who wants it and how badly — the Desirability lens. Supply-side buyer layers answer who will pay for it and whether the economics hold — the Viability lens. Feasibility runs across all three as an honest read on what current AI capability can actually deliver past the trust and liability gate.

The join between the lenses is a simple cost-out / value-in typing. Any given opportunity is a (stage × segment × buyer-layer) cell with a disruption type and a DVF profile. The synthesis builds the map and culls the dead cells; the assessment scores what survives.

The opportunity logic

Demand-side AI is where attention and dissatisfaction concentrate — Desirability is strong, but Viability and Feasibility are weak at booking. Supply-side AI is where money and ROI concentrate — Viability and Feasibility are strong, and Desirability is a sale to a rational buyer rather than a consumer longing.

The best opportunities sit where a demand-side pain meets a supply-side buyer willing to pay to solve it.

03

Five disruption themes

Read together, these are the load-bearing patterns the evidence keeps returning to. Each is the resolution of several working hypotheses, and each carries a confidence rating.

Theme 1 — The planning / booking split is the spine of the market Conf · High

Adoption is mainstream and accelerating upstream — roughly 56% of US travellers have used AI for a trip and generative AI is about a third of research — but it collapses at the transaction. Around 66% will not let AI book, 68% still prefer a trusted brand to complete the purchase, and Klook has measured an 89-point gap between willingness to plan with AI and willingness to book with it. OpenAI pulling in-chat checkout in March 2026 is the same fracture line showing up in a frontier lab's own product decisions. This single split determines which opportunities are real now and which are gated; almost everything else is a corollary of it.

Theme 2 — This is re-intermediation, not disintermediation Conf · High

Agentic search — Google's AI Mode and UCP, ChatGPT and the Agentic Commerce Protocol — inserts a new surface above the booking layer, but routes the transaction through incumbents who keep merchant-of-record status, payment rails, inventory aggregation and post-booking support. The visible "research tax" interface is exposed to competition; the commission pool is defended. Value migrates to whoever owns the new top surface and to whoever owns the defensible rails underneath — and is squeezed out of the undifferentiated middle of thin planners and metasearch.

Theme 3 — Supply-side B2B is where AI already pays its way Conf · Medium-High

Revenue management (hotel ADR/RevPAR up 10–15%; McKinsey's 3–10% per year; airline RM up 3.5–20%), service automation (around 30% cost reduction, roughly two-thirds of contacts automated, 15% upsell), and operations (SITA/OCCam cutting disruption cost by about 30%) show concrete, quantified ROI today. This is cost-out executed as efficiency rather than new demand creation — but it is bankable, measurable, and nearer-term than any consumer play. The principal caveat is that several of the uplift figures are vendor-reported and rated down accordingly.

Theme 4 — Defensibility is structural, not model-based Conf · High

The model layer is commoditising: a thin wrapper can write a serviceable itinerary, and raw LLMs score in the low single digits to ~10% on hard travel-planning benchmarks against roughly 77% for purpose-built systems. Durable advantage therefore comes from proprietary supply and inventory, clean machine-readable data, transactional history (Sabre's ~50 petabytes), and merchant-of-record liability rails. The corollary is the long-tail risk: fragmented supply without modern APIs becomes invisible to agents — which is simultaneously a disruption threat to those suppliers and an aggregation opportunity for whoever cleans the data.

Theme 5 — Trust, liability and capability are the binding constraints Conf · High

Moffatt v. Air Canada — where the carrier was held liable for its chatbot's hallucinated advice — turned AI error into a balance-sheet risk rather than mere UX friction. The moments of highest latent willingness-to-pay, above all in-trip disruption recovery, are exactly where AI needs live booking and PNR access to act rather than merely advise. Where AI only has to advise — planning, RM, service triage — it ships. Where it must transact or act with consequences, it stalls. These constraints gate value-in opportunities more tightly than cost-out ones.

The shape in one line

AI's impact is steeply graded along the journey and inverted between demand and supply: loud-but-shallow for travellers, quiet-but-deep for operators. The opportunity gradient runs from "loud and unmonetised" at the top of the funnel to "quiet and bankable" on the supply side.

06

The six opportunity zones

Each zone is scored 1–5 on Desirability, Viability and Feasibility, with a confidence rating tied back to the evidence base. Desirability asks whether there is a real, felt demand and a willingness to pay. Viability asks whether the economic case holds — who pays, the pricing and margin logic, defensibility and pool size. Feasibility asks, honestly, whether it can be built and delivered with current AI capability, past the trust and liability gate.

Desirability. Suppliers actively buy this now and ROI is the sales pitch — hotels and airlines have both budget and urgency. This is desirability as a sale to a rational buyer, the most reliable kind, rather than a consumer longing.

Viability. Quantified ROI (ADR/RevPAR +10–15%; McKinsey 3–10%/yr; airline RM +3.5–20%; service −30% cost, +15% upsell) on B2B SaaS margins. Two caveats sharpen this. First, the uplift figures are vendor-reported and rated ≤Medium — if real-world uplift runs at half the claim, supplier willingness-to-pay compresses. Second, "AI revenue management" is a feature war inside a consolidated category (IDeaS, Duetto, Amadeus, Sabre, PROS), not greenfield — the open ground is the mid-market and the long-tail of suppliers those enterprise vendors under-serve. RM is the defensible half, anchored in proprietary demand data; the service half has low defensibility, with the moat in data and integration rather than the bot.

Feasibility. Already deployed at scale — Booking's Smart Messenger handles roughly 250k messages a day; Accor and Hyatt are live; SITA/OCCam ships in operations. No trust wall, because the AI optimises or triages while a human or system stays in control. The highest feasibility in the set.

Read. Tier 1 — and the firmer of the two Tier-1 anchors: bankable today and robust even if the trust gap closes. Win the mid-market and long-tail rather than trying to displace IDeaS/Duetto at the enterprise top, and read the $50–250M-ARR SOM as a credible ceiling for a well-funded leader, not a base-case plan. Split the bet: RM is defensible and premium; service is volume and commoditising.

Desirability. Clear, felt pain — no policy enforcement, manual expense, no duty-of-care — and an organisation that already pays for software. Navan Edge exists precisely because this demand is real. Desirability. The highest-satisfaction moment in the evidence — 95% found in-trip AI helpful, 51% used it — and the moment of greatest acute pain, so the highest latent WTP. 08

Directional sizing

These are order-of-magnitude sizes for a go/no-go gate, not a bottoms-up operating model. The one non-negotiable discipline: where a pool is gross booking value (GBV), it is converted to a revenue pool via an explicit take-rate before any obtainable share is drawn — gross bookings are never compared to revenue. TAM is the total annual pool the opportunity could theoretically touch; SAM is the slice realistically serviceable now; SOM is a credible ~3-year capture for a focused category leader, expressed as ARR for a software business.

Z2 — Supply-side RM + service tooling Conf · Medium

Two ways to size it: a conservative software-spend frame, and the much larger value-pool behind it.

Assumption chain — TAM (software frame) ≈ $8–12bn/yr hotel RM software ~$2–4bn (anchor) + airline RM / offer-optimisation software ~$3–4bn (estimate: subset of GDS/airline-IT spend — Amadeus Air IT ~€2.3bn, Sabre IT ~$0.55bn, plus the PROS/Fetcherr/Accelya class) + travel customer-service automation ~$3–4bn (estimate: AI share of travel contact-centre spend).
Cross-check — value pool ≈ $70bn+/yr of revenue uplift created airline passenger revenue ~$693bn × ~3.5% AI-RM uplift ≈ $24bn; global hotel/lodging revenue (est. ~$1.3–1.6tn) × 3–10% McKinsey lift ≈ $40–150bn. A vendor captures only a slice of value created (~5–15% via SaaS/success-fee), which reconciles to the $8–12bn software TAM. (IATA mid-year 2025 puts passenger revenue at ~$693bn, of a $979bn total.)

SAM ≈ $3–5bn/yr — the AI-native, agentic-RM-and-service slice addressable to mid-and-large suppliers ready to adopt now, ~30–40% of TAM. SOM ≈ $50–250M ARR for a focused category leader over ~3 years, capturing ~1–5% of SAM. RM is the defensible half (proprietary demand data); service automation is lower-moat and should expect margin pressure. The most bankable zone — split the bet between defensible RM and commoditising service.

Z1 — SME / unmanaged self-serve corporate AI Conf · Low-Medium

Assumption chain — TAM (revenue pool) ≈ $11–22bn/yr unmanaged corporate travel $544bn GBV × ~2–4% blended platform take (SaaS subscription + transaction fee + expense automation) = $11–22bn. Cross-check: total T&E software ~$4–5bn today → ~$13.5bn by 2032; the SME/unmanaged slice (~40%) implies a near-term software-only SAM of ~$2–5bn.

SAM ≈ $2–5bn/yr — the share reachable by an AI-enabled self-serve product now (digitally mature SMEs, supported geographies), ~15–25% of TAM. SOM ≈ $100–300M ARR over ~3–5 years. The anchor: Navan reached $702M revenue (FY2026, +31%) with first positive non-GAAP operating income — proof a leader scales. Two cautions from the stress-test: only ~$62M of that $702M is subscription, so the "ARR" here is mostly usage/take-rate, not a software annuity; and a new entrant reaching $100–300M in three years sits at the optimistic end of comparables (Navan is a decade old and raised ~$2.2bn). The key uncertainty remains the GBV→take conversion (a different take rate moves the pool ~2×), and the AI-specific WTP is inferred rather than measured — flagged.

Z4 — Long-tail supply aggregation Conf · Low

Assumption chain — TAM (revenue pool) ≈ $18–46bn/yr online tours & activities reservations ~$185bn GBV × ~10–25% aggregator take-rate. (Broader "experiences" at ~$360bn would roughly double the ceiling.)

SAM ≈ $4–18bn/yr — the non-API, hard-to-aggregate long-tail that AI tooling specifically unlocks, ~20–40% of the take-rate pool. SOM is small and ops-gated: onboarding messy supply is manual and slow, and incumbents are already consolidating (HBX/Bridgify, May 2026). A focused entrant is plausibly low-tens-of-$M ARR in three years. Owns a defensible data layer if executed, but capital- and ops-intensive — a picks-and-shovels bet.

Z3 — In-trip / disruption-recovery Conf · Low · excluded from gate math

Deliberately left unsized as a base case, because willingness-to-pay is unproven — the study's single biggest evidence gap. Carried as directional upside only.

Illustrative band only — not a forecast — ≈ $1–5bn/yr if ~10% of the 1.54bn international trips carry a ~$10–30 in-trip-assist value (traveller-paid or supplier-funded retention), that is ~$1.5–4.6bn, plus an unquantified supplier-funded "retain the disrupted guest" pool.

SAM and SOM are negligible near-term until the live-PNR / transaction feasibility wall clears. Build the advisory wedge now at low cost, and pressure-test WTP before any sizing is trusted. This number must not enter the go/no-go math except as upside.

Assumption chain — TAM ≈ $0.1–0.5bn/yr global travel advisors in the tens of thousands × ~$1–5k/yr per-seat SaaS for proposal-generation / CRM-synthesis tooling.

SAM ≈ $50–200M; SOM ≈ single-digit $M ARR. High feasibility (pure back-office, no trust wall) but a small ceiling. A reliable niche with strong margins, not a market-maker.

Z5 — Agentic booking rails Conf · Low · scenario only

Sits on the ~$94–107bn OTA commission pool plus airline/hotel direct. As a scenario: if agentic flows eventually re-route even 5–10% of commission value through new rails, that is a ~$5–10bn pool in motion — but value accrues to incumbents who hold merchant-of-record (Google UCP / OpenAI ACP route through Expedia/Booking). Risks & what would break the thesis

The honest version of a go-recommendation names the things that would falsify it. The stress-test (Stage 6) re-verified the four load-bearing anchors against primary sources — the $544bn unmanaged pool, Navan's $702M (SEC-filed), IATA's $979bn total, and the trust gap (raised to High) — and none failed. What follows are the conditions under which the call should still change.

The three falsifiers — monitorable, none evident today

  • Real-world RM / service uplift materially below vendor claims. The Z2 ROI figures (RM +10–15%, service −30%, airline RM +3.5–20%) are largely self-reported by sellers. If true uplift runs at half the claim, supplier willingness-to-pay compresses and the $70bn+ value pool halves. The direction is corroborated by named at-scale deployments — but always read the conservative $8–12bn software frame, never the value pool alone.
  • The SME-AI layer commands no premium over a plain booking tool. Z1's AI-specific WTP is inferred, not measured, and the business is take-rate-monetised (Navan: ~$62M subscription of $702M). If "AI" is just table stakes that every incumbent adds, Z1 is a distribution fight, not a moat — its case weakens to the lower end.
  • The consumer trust gap closes inside ~2 years. OpenAI's March-2026 walkback proves unreadiness, not permanent impossibility. If protocols (ACP/UCP/MCP) and liability frameworks mature fast, the demand-side surface captures more value than assumed and "stay supply-side" looks too conservative — elevating agentic rails (Z5). This is the single variable to monitor; it is the trigger that re-rates the whole map.
Why the GO is robust to its own biggest risk

Even if the trust gap closes early, value still routes through incumbents — the re-intermediation finding (Theme 2). So the supply-side tooling thesis, and Z2 in particular, holds up whether the gap persists or closes; what shifts is only the relative attractiveness of the rails option. That asymmetry is a large part of why Z2 is the firmer anchor of the two Tier-1 bets.

Standing low-confidence flags

  • Z3 has no proven willingness-to-pay. The $1–5bn band is illustrative and excluded from the gate math; this is the most important single number not to over-trust. Build the advisory wedge now; test WTP before it enters any size.
  • Z4 take-rate (10–25%) and long-tail share (20–40%) are both estimates; precision is low, the work is supply ops not modelling, and incumbent consolidation (HBX/Bridgify) could close the window.
  • All software-market anchors are aggregator-sourced with methodology variance, rated Low-Medium — re-validate before any investment-grade use.
Counter-position, stated fairly

The strongest case against the supply-side thesis: cost-out tooling is efficiency, not new demand, so it competes on price and commoditises, while the consumer-planning surface — however unmonetised today — is where the durable category-defining brands may ultimately form. The study's answer is that defensibility in travel is structural (data, inventory, liability rails), not interface-deep, and that a bankable few-hundred-million-ARR business beats a large-but-contested land-grab for an entrant without platform-scale distribution. That is a judgement, and it is the judgement the stress-test should attack.

The recommendation
Verdict · GOon the supply-side B2B + SME self-serve thesis

The Tier-1 case clears the gate — anchored on Z2.

Z2 (~$3–5bn SAM) and Z1 (~$2–5bn SAM) are large enough, have proven buyers and ROI, and a focused entrant can credibly reach low-hundreds-of-$M ARR — without relying on the contested booking transaction. An independent Stage-6 red-team did not break the thesis; it sharpened it. Z2 (AI revenue management for the mid-market and long-tail) is the firmer anchor — its demand holds whether or not the consumer trust gap ever closes. Z1 (SME self-serve travel) is a deliberate, eyes-open fast-follow: real, but more contested, and — on the clearest comparable (Navan books only ~$62M of its ~$702M revenue as subscription) — a take-rate business rather than a software annuity. Read its low-hundreds-of-$M SOM as a credible ceiling, not a base-case plan.

Tier-2 is real optionality, not base case: Z4 (long-tail) has a genuinely large pool but is ops-heavy and unsized with precision; Z3 (in-trip) is the most desirable moment in the market but must not enter the go/no-go math until WTP is tested — it is upside. Tier-3 is a profitable niche (Z6) and a watch-and-position option keyed to the trust gap closing (Z5).

Robust to its own biggest risk. Even if consumers never come to trust AI with the booking, value re-intermediates through the incumbents Z2 and Z1 already sell to — so the GO does not depend on the trust gap closing. The three things that would falsify it (RM/service uplift below vendor claims; SME AI commanding no premium; a sub-two-year trust-gap close) are monitorable and none is evident today.

Method, confidence & sources

This is a DVF (Desirability–Viability–Feasibility) opportunity assessment to Big-4 standard, built on public sources and structured estimation only — no proprietary research. Every quantified claim is logged in the project ledger with its source or estimation method and a confidence rating; vendor-reported figures are flagged and rated down; gross bookings are never compared to revenue. Sizing is directional and order-of-magnitude — built for a go/no-go gate, not a bottoms-up operating model — with assumption chains kept short and visible.

Confidence ratings. High multiple independent or primary sources agree · Medium sound but single-source or definition-sensitive · Low estimated or thin evidence, flagged.
