Travel opportunities in adjacent industries, horizontals and verticals.

A Desirability–Viability–Feasibility assessment of the businesses that sit next to travel — services, software, insurance, media and tooling — scoped for a small, AI-native operator with consultant-scale capital. Companion to the interactive opportunity map.

Method: staged autonomous study · 35 sourced evidence rows + 9 transparent estimates · independent Stage-6 stress-test · public sources and structured estimation only.

Executive summary

The verdict is GO — but not where adjacency-scanning usually looks.

This study scanned every category of business adjacent to selling travel — from marketing agencies and insurance to vertical SaaS, the creator economy and the advisor layer — and asked a single question: where, over the next three to five years, is the most attractive and feasible venture for a small, AI-native operator, and is any zone strong enough for a GO?

The answer turns on a structural shift the evidence supports from multiple independent directions: AI is moving travel's adjacent profit pools from production to legibility and judgment. Production — content volume, tier-one servicing, SEO-era discoverability — is being costed out: travel executives already report realised AI cost reductions, and Expedia's agent alone absorbs 143 million conversations a year. What is being created is a new, budgeted set of problems. The overlap between Google rankings and AI-engine citations collapsed from 76% to between 17% and 38% in a matter of months, meaning twenty years of SEO equity does not reliably transfer to the channel where a third of travellers now begin research. Suppliers must simultaneously become legible to AI agents — the Sabre/PayPal/Mindtrip pipeline launches this quarter across 420+ airlines and two million hotel properties — while 80% of executives plan agentic deployment that only 2% of consumers yet trust. Visibility, agent-readiness, and governance are forced, board-visible problems with no entrenched service incumbent.

Consumer-facing adjacencies, by contrast, are closed: fintech and insurance behind licences and flows, planning apps behind a distribution wall investors have explicitly abandoned (B2B funding overtook B2C in 2024 for the first time), and experiences demand-aggregation behind funded OTAs. The travel-funding drought — a decade low, with 83% of capital going to 6% of companies — is hostile to venture-scale entry but favourable to cash-flow businesses: niches contain fewer subsidised competitors than at any point since 2016.

The recommended play is a sequenced B2B "legibility stack": an AI-visibility (GEO) practice for travel brands as the anchor (scored 5-4-5 on DVF), paired with travel-specialised AI strategy and governance advisory (4-3-5), distributed through an owned intelligence wedge, with agentic-readiness consulting and a productised diagnostic/benchmark gated behind explicit triggers. Directionally, the portfolio supports A$1.1–3.0M of revenue within three years on under A$250k of capital. The verdict survived an independent red-team with one material adjustment: viability evidence for the anchor zone rests partly on young, aggregator-grade GEO market forecasts, so the GO carries four monitorable falsifiers rather than unconditional confidence.

The strategic question

What the study had to answer.

Central questionWhere, across the industries adjacent to travel, is the most attractive and feasible venture opportunity for a small, AI-native operator over the next 3–5 years — and is any zone strong enough for a GO?

Scope: global; all adjacency types (B2B services to travel companies, consumer travel-adjacent products, tech and platforms serving travel, travel verticals inside horizontal industries); explicitly excluding the launch of a core travel seller, which is the territory of a separate venture-feasibility study and treated only as context here. Six sub-questions structured the work: where value is migrating in the adjacent chain; whether a defensible services business exists; where vertical software and AI gaps remain unclosed; which consumer adjacencies have attractive economics; who the buyer is and what they pay; and which zones are enterable with under ~A$250k, no inventory, and one to three people.

Findings · disruption themes

Seven forces, one direction.

T1 — The discoverability reset

The single most important finding in the corpus. Ahrefs measured the overlap between Google top-10 rankings and AI Overview citations at 76% in July 2025 and 38% by early 2026 across 863,000 keywords; BrightEdge's parallel methodology found roughly 17%; ChatGPT's overlap with Google's top ten runs at 7–12%. The numbers disagree on magnitude but agree on direction and volatility — which is exactly the condition under which brands buy external help. The first Airlines & Hotels AI Visibility Index (5W, 2026 — vendor-produced and flagged accordingly) found earned media and structured third-party authority dominate citation share, the top three brands capture over 70% of citations in several sub-categories, and luxury hotel brands — the highest-margin buyers in hospitality — measure structurally under-cited relative to their pricing power. A third-plus of US travellers reportedly begin research in AI engines, and 55% used AI in trip planning in 2025, with extensive use up 124% year on year.

T2 — The agentic plumbing wave

MCP has become the connective standard between AI assistants and travel systems, adopted or built against by Booking.com, Expedia, Turkish Airlines, Amadeus and Sabre. Sabre shipped agentic-ready APIs and an MCP server in September 2025, demonstrated end-to-end conversational booking at CES in January 2026, and its partnership with PayPal and Mindtrip launches the industry's first end-to-end agentic booking pipeline in Q2 2026. Amadeus publicly argues MCP is necessary but insufficient and is pushing a commerce-layer standard (UCP) — contested standards being themselves a source of advisory demand. AI-driven web traffic grew 187% in 2025 with over 95% concentrated in retail, media and travel. The GDS vendors sell the rails; the work of making an individual travel business agent-legible — and deciding what not to expose — is unclaimed.

T3 — Cost-out commoditises production, not judgment

In McKinsey's survey of travel executives, 26% report realised AI cost reductions and 59% productivity gains, with a majority reporting more than 6% annual cost savings. Expedia's AI agent handles 143 million conversations a year with over half of travellers self-serving. The implication for adjacent services: anything priced on deliverable volume — content production, tier-one support, generic campaign execution — deflates with its buyer's costs. Services priced on judgment, accountability and outcomes hold their pricing.

T4 — Barbell capital

Travel startup funding sits at decade lows: $5.3B (2023), $5.8B (2024), $3.5B through 3Q25 and under $5B for the full year, against the $16.3B peak of 2021. Deal count collapsed from roughly a thousand rounds a year to 161 through 3Q25; only ~260 travel companies raised in 2025 versus 1,077 in 2018, and 83% of all funding goes to 6% of companies. B2B funding overtook B2C for the first time in 2024. Venture-scale entry is hostile; cash-flow niches are emptier of subsidised competitors than at any time in a decade.

T5 — The judgment premium professionalises

Luxury travel yields are holding while mainstream yields decline. Fora reached $2.3 billion in cumulative sales (gross bookings, not revenue) in five years on just $73.5M raised — crossing $1B around April 2025 and adding roughly the next $1.3B within about twelve months — and is acquiring AI tooling for advisor workflow. Human curation is migrating upmarket, professionalising, and under-buying software, marketing and intelligence.

T6 — Embedded everything

Travel insurance is a $22–31B global market growing at 11–18% annually, but distribution is consolidating into embedded, API-placed products inside booking flows (Chubb's parametric Travel Pro embedded in airline and OTA paths). Value accrues to underwriters and flow-owners; everyone else is an affiliate. The same logic closes consumer FX and payments.

T7 — The under-digitised long tail

The experiences sector reached $271B in gross bookings in 2025, heading to $342B by 2029 — and only 33% of it is booked online (versus 64% for travel overall), across roughly a million operators, more than 70% of them small or very small. Enormous and real, but profitably serving micro-operators is a product-and-distribution game already contested by funded platforms.

Risks

What could break this.

Efficacy risk (Z1). GEO is young enough that nobody has proven citation share is reliably movable. If two to three quarters of earned-media and structured-authority work doesn't move client numbers, retainers collapse to one-off audits. Mitigation: price the first cohort on audits plus performance-framed retainers; instrument everything.

Platform risk. If AI platforms monetise answer placement (ads inside answers), organic visibility services shrink toward media buying — a different, scale-favouring business. Mitigation: keep the practice channel-agnostic (visibility = earned + structured + paid as it emerges).

Timing risk (Z2). Consumer trust in autonomous booking is 2%; if agentic pipelines don't shift measurable volume by mid-2027, the urgency narrative deflates. That is why Z2 is gated, not built.

Competition risk. Big-4, GDS professional-services arms and PR conglomerates (5W is already publishing travel visibility research) will move in as categories mature. The defence is sequencing: vertical authority and benchmark data established before they arrive.

Measurement risk. Citation-overlap statistics swing wildly between methodologies and months; a practice built on a number that stabilises favourably for incumbents loses its founding story. Mitigation: own the measurement (Z4) rather than rent the narrative.

Verdict

GO — on the B2B legibility stack.

Build the AI-visibility practice (Z1) as the anchor, paired with travel-specialised AI strategy and governance advisory (Z3), distributed through an owned intelligence wedge (Z7). Gate agentic-readiness (Z2) on demonstrated booking volume through the 2026 pipelines, and gate the productised diagnostic (Z4) on five to eight completed service engagements. Watch the advisor economy (Z5) and the experiences long tail (Z6). Do not enter licensed, flow-owned or consumer-distribution categories.

Confidence: moderate-high on direction, moderate on magnitude. The desirability and feasibility evidence for the anchor is strong and multi-sourced; viability rests partly on young, aggregator-grade market forecasts, which is why the GO carries falsifiers rather than certainty.

Stage 6 · independent stress-test

Red-team review.

An independent review pass re-verified the gate-critical anchor numbers from fresh search and attacked the thesis. Note on method: this environment could not spawn a fully separate agent, so independence was approximated by a clean-context re-verification of anchors and an adversarial pass conducted against, not from, the study documents. Findings:

Numbers stress-test. Anchor figures re-confirmed within rounding: travel funding decade-low and 83/6 concentration (Phocuswright); citation-overlap collapse (Ahrefs 863k-keyword study; BrightEdge parallel); Navan S-1 figures (CNBC); Fora $2.3B (Skift); experiences $271B/33% online (Arival–Phocuswright); global bookings $1.67T (Phocuswright). Two figures were rated down during the study and remain down: the GEO market size (aggregator-grade) and the 5W Index findings (vendor-produced, self-interested). One pre-existing unledgered corpus found in the workspace was quarantined at Stage 1 and used only as a hypothesis list; all retained claims were independently re-sourced.

Strongest counter-arguments. (1) GEO may be SEO rebranded, and the overlap statistics may stabilise — overlap numbers moved 76→38→17% within months on methodology changes; a practice must own measurement, not rent a scary statistic. Accepted: folded into risk and Z4 gating. (2) The buyer may become the competitor — large hotel groups and OTAs will in-house visibility work as tooling matures; the durable client set is the mid-market and luxury independent segment plus DMOs, which is consistent with Z1's targeting. (3) Agencies with media scale (5W et al.) can outrun a boutique — true at enterprise tier; the boutique defence is vertical depth + governance credentials they lack, and speed. (4) The A$1.1–3.0M envelope is a consulting income, not a venture — accepted as framing: this is a cash-flow venture with a productised option (Z4), which matches the operator's stated capital and model, not a venture-scale claim.

Confidence-adjusted verdict. GO confirmed, with the explicit condition that Z1 is run as a falsifiable experiment for its first two to three quarters (audit-led, instrumented), not as a committed multi-year build. The four falsifiers on the hero map are the monitoring plan: citation-share movability, paid-placement encroachment, agentic booking volume by mid-2027, and incumbent productisation by 2027.

Method & sources

How the study was built.

Seven-stage autonomous DVF process: frame (one scoping round) → gather (fresh web evidence, four lenses) → synthesise (value chain, themes, thesis) → opportunities × DVF → directional sizing → deliverables → independent stress-test. Evidence discipline: every quantified or load-bearing claim carries a ledger row tagged SOURCED (with link) or ESTIMATED (with assumption and method) and an H/M/L confidence rating; vendor-reported figures flagged and rated down; gross bookings never compared to revenue. Public sources and structured estimation only. Sizing is order-of-magnitude, built for a gate.
